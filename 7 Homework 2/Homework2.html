I investigated the relationship between leprosy cases per 100K people and GDP per capita. The analysis revealed a weak negative correlation between the two variables, indicating that countries with lower GDP per capita tend to have higher leprosy cases per 100K people. This suggests that wealthier countries are likely to have better healthcare infrastructure, which can contribute to lower leprosy prevalence. However, there are notable outliers, such as Brazil, where despite having a higher GDP, the leprosy cases remain significantly elevated. This points to the complexity of leprosy distribution, which is influenced by multiple factors beyond just economic indicators. The correlation coefficient was about -0.06, which suggests a slight negative correlation
